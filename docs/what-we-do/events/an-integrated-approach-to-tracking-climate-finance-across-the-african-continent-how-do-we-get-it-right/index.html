An integrated approach to tracking climate finance across the African continent: How do we get it right?

As part of the Tenth Session of the Africa Regional Forum on Sustainable Development, DI and PACJA are looking deeper into tracking public climate finance.

  • 18 April 2024
  • 4–6pm
  • UN Economic Commission for Africa, Addis Ababa
Register

Development Initiatives (DI) and the Pan African Climate Justice Alliance (PACJA) are organising a joint side event to offer insight into the issues with tracking public climate finance. Speakers from African governments, civil society, UN and Africa Union will share their perspectives and experiences on the challenges and opportunities for increasing the transparency and accountability of climate finance in order to measure progress.

Our expert panellists will lead a conversation around developing tracking methodologies that can create better and more consistent reporting. They will consider practical recommendations to ensure that Africa’s negotiating positions at the forum are supported by data and evidence.

Anyone participating in the regional forum is welcome, no need to register. If you have any questions or accessibility requirements, please contact Martha Bekele, DI’s Delivery, Quality & Impact Lead .

Background to this event

The failure to address the worsening impacts of climate change is already contributing to environmental, socioeconomic and political disruption on the African continent. It is globally accepted that those least responsible for the climate crisis are facing the largest and increasingly devastating impacts.

Poor, inconsistent reporting of climate finance and its separation from international development and humanitarian flows undermines the ability of climate, development and crisis advocates to track the delivery of commitments and hold donors to account. It significantly curtails the visibility that climate-vulnerable governments have of flows coming into their country, their ability to plan and manage such flows, and to advocate for more and better financing. It also limits donors’ assessments of the sufficiency of the total scale of international effort against need and the potential for greater coherence across projects, programmes and actors. Moreover, enhanced tracking and reporting will help create a picture of how funding and coordination are plotted across the development and humanitarian nexus, revealing gaps for the most vulnerable people and possible opportunities to strengthen the inputs of local and national actors.

Without a better understanding of climate finance and how it sits with development and humanitarian flows, we cannot achieve much-needed transparency, accountability or coherence, ultimately limiting the effectiveness and impact of climate and other forms of associated international finance.

Climate finance is targeted primarily at state-led priorities identified under the UNFCCC. In the context of protracted crisis, particularly, humanitarian finance is increasingly stretched to respond to climate impacts and build resilience, exacerbated by the lack of coordination. Yet, even where coordination is possible, large funding gaps in the humanitarian and climate sectors make comprehensive coverage a challenge, particularly for funding for fragile and conflict-affected states. Recent analysis by Development Initiatives shows that countries facing protracted crisis that are highly climate-vulnerable have received only US$1 of climate funding per person over the last 20 years .

An increase in climate finance is vital for effective and fair climate action, and will be pursued under the New Collective Quantified Goal on Climate Finance . This is expected to avert, minimise and address the impacts of climate change, and hopefully it will incentivise greater action all round. However, it is impossible to hold countries to account if their spending cannot be measured. Without a standardised approach to accounting and accountability, those most in need will continue to be short-changed.
